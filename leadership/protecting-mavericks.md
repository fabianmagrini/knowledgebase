# Protecting Mavericks

**Mavericks** are independent thinkers and challengers of the status quo — often the source
of breakthroughs and a guard against organisational stagnation. Their unconventional
approach also creates friction. Protecting them is the management discipline of letting
their talent flourish without letting their disruption damage the team.

This is the *people* counterpart to [managed disruption](managed-disruption.md): managed
disruption channels change safely through process and architecture; protecting mavericks
channels it safely through individuals.

## Who they are

Non-conformist, purpose-driven, willing to take calculated risks and question established
norms. Frequently high performers. Their resistance to authority and conventional structure
can make them appear disruptive.

## The double-edged sword

- **Value:** they break groupthink, question assumptions, and surface game-changing ideas.
- **Cost:** disregard for process creates friction; blunt communication reads as abrasive;
  independence clashes with team-oriented environments.

## Strategies

- **Psychological safety** — make dissent safe; frame failure as learning.
- **Autonomy and ownership** — give freedom to explore; be flexible with rules that stifle
  creativity.
- **Clear goals and boundaries** — autonomy is not a free-for-all; align effort to strategy
  and contain negative spillover onto the team.
- **Support and recognition** — champion their contributions publicly so peers understand
  their role.
- **Coaching, not conformity** — develop communication and collaboration skills rather than
  forcing them into a mould.
- **Manage team dynamics** — handle the frustrations of colleagues who find their style
  challenging.
- **Trust-based relationships** — mavericks respond to managers they respect; openness and
  honesty are foundational.

## Bottom line

Protecting mavericks is not shielding them from all rules and consequences. It is building
an environment where unconventional talent flourishes for the benefit of the whole
organisation.
