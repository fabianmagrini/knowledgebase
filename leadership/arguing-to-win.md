# Why Arguing to Win Fails

Cong Wang describes abandoning the habit of arguing to prove himself correct, framing
it as a technical mindset misapplied to people. His argument is that winning an
argument is close to worthless: establishing that you are right also **manufactures
someone visibly wrong**, creating a loser where there did not need to be one. He
reaches for *Tao Te Ching* ch. 2 — opposites define each other — to make the point
that "correct" is not automatically "good".

## Why the logic bounces off

- **Most disputes are ego, not ideas.** When someone's identity is tied to a position,
  they defend it the way anyone defends themselves — "not with reason, but with
  resistance." The stronger your logic, the harder the defence.
- **People are emotional-first reasoners.** They "feel first, then reason backward to
  justify the feeling", so a better argument rarely moves them.
- **Correction rarely teaches.** People learn from consequences, not advice: "words
  bounce off; pain sticks."

## The heuristics he adopted

| Heuristic | What it means |
|---|---|
| **The Clean Exception** | Offer help or correction only when it is explicitly requested; unsolicited correction lands as an attack, not a gift. |
| **Profit from disagreement** | Treat a genuine difference of view as a potential edge (a "moat") to act on, rather than something to debate down to consensus. |
| **Change yourself** | The only reliably changeable variable is your own behaviour; invest energy in seeking feedback and improving rather than in converting others. |
