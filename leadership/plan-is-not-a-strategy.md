# A Plan Is Not a Strategy

Roger Martin argues that most organisations confuse **planning** with **strategy**.
What they call a "strategic plan" is usually a list of initiatives — *improve customer
experience, launch a new product, hire more people, open an office*. Those are
activities. They describe what the organisation will *do*, not how it will *win*.

His definition of strategy:

> An integrative set of choices that positions you on a chosen playing field in a way
> that allows you to win.

A real strategy answers two questions that must cohere into a single theory of why
customers choose you over the alternatives:

1. **Where will we play?**
2. **How will we win?**

## Why planning feels safer

| | Planning | Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **Concerns** | Budgets, headcount, projects, facilities, resources | Customer behaviour, competitor reactions, market shifts |
| **Control** | Things you control | Things you do **not** control |
| **Feel** | Comfortable, measurable | Uncomfortable — a hypothesis that may be wrong |

Because leaders control the inputs to a plan, planning feels safe and trackable.
Strategy is uncomfortable precisely because it is a **bet about the future**: leaders
must choose despite uncertainty rather than wait for perfect information.

## The Southwest example

While other airlines competed against each other with hub-and-spoke networks,
Southwest chose a different playing field — it competed with *car and bus travel*. That
single choice (point-to-point routes, low cost, affordable and convenient flying) then
shaped every operational decision. It was a coherent way to win, not a collection of
initiatives.

## Avoiding the planning trap

1. **Accept uncertainty.** Strategy is informed betting, not certainty.
2. **Make explicit choices.** Decide where you will compete *and where you won't* —
   don't try to be everything to everyone.
3. **State and test your assumptions.** What must be true about customers, competitors,
   and our own capabilities? Treat those as testable, and revisit them over time.

## Applied to engineering leadership

A technology roadmap is usually a **plan**: *migrate to cloud, adopt microservices,
implement AI agents, improve developer experience, raise test coverage*. A **strategy**
answers different questions:

- Where will we create differentiated value?
- Which engineering capabilities matter most?
- What are we deliberately *not* investing in?
- How do these choices help us win in our market?

The distinction Martin draws:

- ❌ *"Implement AI coding tools."* — an initiative.
- ✅ *"Use AI to double delivery throughput while maintaining governance, so we outpace
  competitors on feature delivery."* — a where-to-play / how-to-win choice. The tooling
  is one line of the plan that *serves* the strategy.

**One-sentence takeaway:** planning is a list of activities you control; strategy is a
coherent set of choices designed to achieve outcomes you don't.
