# Managing Sideways

Kevin Goldsmith argues that most of a senior leader's real work is **sideways, not up** —
driving change across peer teams you don't formally control. The failure it addresses is
familiar: a well-reasoned proposal wins unanimous agreement in the meeting, then nothing
changes, because *"agreeing was free and changing was not."* Getting a nod costs a team
nothing; actually changing how they work competes with their own urgent priorities.

## Why authority fails

| Approach | Why it backfires |
|---|---|
| **Borrowed authority** (escalate to a senior leader) | Produces shallow, temporary compliance that evaporates when the pressure lifts — and teams learn to oppose you pre-emptively next time |
| **Direct authority** (use your own power) | Even CTOs hit diminishing returns; forced compliance breeds resentment and erodes trust |

> "Being right is the cheapest part."

Technical soundness is disconnected from willingness to adopt. The adopting team optimises for
*its own* urgent needs, not the logic of your initiative — so a direct technical debate usually
misses the actual concern.

## Adoption without a mandate

Goldsmith's sequential four-move play:

1. **Recruit the first mover.** Start with a team that already wants the change, not the
   sceptics. Momentum comes from willing adopters.
2. **Frame around *their* work reduction.** Emphasise how it solves their problem and helps
   *their* metrics — not how it advances your initiative.
3. **Let results recruit the next.** Visible peer success persuades better than advocacy —
   *"proof travels further than persuasion."*
4. **Settle ownership before scaling.** Establish governance and arbitration before adoption
   spreads, to avoid tragedy-of-the-commons dynamics.

## Resistance is fear, not logic

Resistance usually stems from threats to **autonomy, job security, or professional identity**,
not from flaws in your reasoning. Goldsmith's example: developer resistance to AI tooling is
often fear of losing a *coding identity*, not a judgement about the tool — so arguing the tool's
merits misses the point. (See [Scaling AI Adoption](../engineering/practices/scaling-ai-adoption.md)
for the organisational version of this change-adoption problem.)

## When a mandate makes sense

Top-down directives are the right call when **urgency is extreme, the timeline is tight, failure
risk is high, and alignment is low** — accept shallow buy-in as the cost of speed. Otherwise,
invest in influence.

## The diagnostic

Name the one team that would adopt your change immediately, with **zero persuasion**. If you've
been fighting sceptics instead, that's the signal you're pushing the wrong first mover. If *no*
such team exists, the change doesn't yet solve anyone's real problem.
