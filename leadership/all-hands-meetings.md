# Running an Effective All-Hands

Michael Lopp (Rands) argues the all-hands exists to counter **organisational information
decay**: as a group grows past roughly 50 people, "the ebb and flow of information decreases as
a function of population size," and the organic knowledge-sharing that worked in a small team
stops reaching everyone. A well-run all-hands is the venue that guarantees critical context
lands with the whole team — especially newcomers, who lack the informal networks established
staff use to gather signal. The goal is for people to think *"this is where I will learn,"*
inoculating the org against gossip, rumours, and lies.

Lopp asserts that **at least half** of teams see their all-hands as a waste — because the
information feels redundant, the content centres leadership rather than the team, or the
delivery is dull. (The 50% figure is his estimate, not a measured statistic.)

## The structure — three acts, 90 minutes max

| Act | ~Time | Content |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | 30 min | Brief welcome + a **"Tree Talk"** — teach something unrelated but curiosity-driven — and a *single* org-chart slide showing what changed since last time |
| **2** | 30 min | Direct reports present (7–10 min each): what they built, learned, or how their team changed. Rotate if more than three reports |
| **3** | 30 min | A **mystery guest** interview with pre-planned questions; keep the guest's identity secret to boost attendance |

## Critical practices

- **Consistency over novelty.** *"Pick a format for your All Hands and stick with it."*
  Predictability reduces anxiety — a randomly-called or reformatted meeting makes people fear
  layoffs or trouble.
- **Quarterly cadence.** At least once a quarter; monthly is "overkill," annual is insufficient.
- **No live Q&A.** In a large group, questions feel random or self-serving. Route them to other
  channels (Slack, staff meetings) instead.
- **Delegate the stage.** The leader should present minimally — *"this is not your show."*
  Developing direct reports' communication skills matters more than a polished performance.
