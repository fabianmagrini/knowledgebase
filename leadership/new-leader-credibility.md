# The New Leader's Credibility Problem

Michael Lopp (Rands) describes the paradox a newly-hired senior leader faces: you
are brought in to find what is broken, you are even told what is broken — and your
diagnosis **will not be believed**, especially the parts that are uncomfortable.
His argument is that credibility is earned through **systematic investigation, not
declaration**: belief follows demonstrated understanding, not the authority of the
title.

> "You'll know it's working when they don't believe you."

The fresh perspective of a new leader is the asset — unclouded by the
organisation's history — but it is also why the diagnosis lands as unwelcome. The
leaders already in place are "in the centre of the chaos", their perspective
blurred by it.

## Find the mystery first

Lopp's prescription is to **listen before solving**. His illustrative case: a new
VP of Engineering at a ~110-person company spent the first month largely quiet,
gathering intelligence, which made an anxious co-founder demand visibility. When
the VP eventually presented discovered problems at an All Hands, the CEO dismissed
the assessment and asked him to focus on the *CEO's* preferred issues instead —
exactly the reaction the thesis predicts.

The work of the early months is to **find the mystery** — the real problem beneath
the stated ones — before proposing solutions. A related trap is the
**manager-echo-chamber**: hearing only the management layer's version and mistaking
it for the whole picture.

## The communication cascade

Credibility is built by listening across widening circles, not by a single
announcement:

| Forum | Who |
|---|---|
| **Recurring 1:1s** | The inner circle — direct reports, your boss, influential individuals |
| **Extended staff** | All managers plus senior non-manager leaders |
| **Staff** | Direct reports only |
| **All Hands** | The entire team |

Each layer is both a listening post and, later, a channel for communicating what
you have learned.

## Ownership

A recurring point: no one will hand you a mandate. *"It's no one else's job but
yours to fix what ails your team. No one is going to give you permission."* The
credibility problem is not an obstacle to route around but the job itself —
demonstrated understanding is what eventually converts skepticism into trust.
