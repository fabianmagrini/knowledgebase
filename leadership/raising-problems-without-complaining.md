# Raising Problems Without Complaining

Managers tend to interpret complaints differently from how they are intended. When an engineer
repeatedly cites external blockers, a manager often hears a gap in ownership and collaboration
rather than a legitimate obstacle. The gap between the signal sent and the signal received is what
shapes how an engineer is perceived over time — and, with it, their trajectory.

## Pattern recognition

A manager distinguishes an occasional hard situation from a persistent pattern of external blame:
"there's a difference between running into difficult people occasionally, and running into
difficult people everywhere." One frustrated moment reads as circumstance; a recurring theme reads
as a perception or skill problem on the complainer's side.

## What managers notice

| Signal | Reads as competence | Reads as dependency |
|---|---|---|
| **Self-help effort** | "I'm stuck on X — here's what I've tried" | "Y team hasn't responded" |
| **Ease of response** | Provides context and a clear ask, so helping costs others almost nothing | Makes others do the work of figuring out what is needed |
| **Direct conversation first** | Attempts peer-level resolution before escalating | Escalates to the manager first — reads as junior thinking or conflict avoidance |

Removing friction for others is part of the job: making stakeholder engagement easy is the
engineer's responsibility too, not only the stakeholder's.

## The core move

Before expecting others to change, ask: **"What's my move here?"** This shifts the stance from
passive complaint to active problem-solving — the difference between engineers who wait for better
conditions and those who create them.

## Diagnose the real blocker

A worked example: an engineer blamed a PM for changing specs, but investigation showed business
stakeholders were driving the changes. Once those stakeholders were educated, the problem resolved
— the engineer had not diagnosed the actual blocker. Complaining about the visible symptom can
mask the real cause.

## Anti-pattern

Absorbing frustration and then venting to a manager in a 1-on-1 — without first attempting a
direct peer conversation or understanding the root cause.
